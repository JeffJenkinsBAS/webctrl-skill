# Airside Systems Reference

Deep reference for VAV AHU resets, VAV box cascade control, DOAS, energy recovery, FCUs, lab exhaust/fume hoods, and higher-ed load profiles. Read alongside the Reset Strategy Guidance and Psychrometric Process Decision Guidance tables in SKILL.md.

## VAV Air Handling Units — Supply Air Temperature (SAT) Reset

SAT reset raises the AHU discharge setpoint as cooling demand allows (either based on outdoor air temperature or, more effectively, based on zone valve/damper demand, per ASHRAE Guideline 36 trim-and-respond logic), reducing reheat energy and simultaneous heating/cooling. This mirrors the CHWST reset logic (see `chiller-plants.md`) — both use the same trim-and-respond principle of watching the "worst" (most-demanding) zone and backing off setpoint only as far as that zone allows.

## Static Pressure Reset / Trim-and-Respond

**Trim-and-Respond (T&R)** static pressure reset is the ASHRAE Guideline 36 standard sequence for VAV fan control ([ASHRAE Addendum h to Guideline 36-2021](https://www.ashrae.org/file%20library/technical%20resources/standards%20and%20guidelines/standards%20addenda/g36_2021_h_20240229.pdf)):

- Each VAV box damper position is monitored; when a box damper is nearly/fully open (i.e., it cannot get enough air at the current duct static pressure), it generates a "request" for more pressure.
- If ≥1 requests are outstanding, the duct static pressure setpoint is *trimmed up* (increased) by a small increment.
- If 0 requests are outstanding for a defined interval, the setpoint is *trimmed down* (decreased) by a (typically larger) decrement.
- This "hunts" toward the minimum static pressure that satisfies the most demanding zone(s), continuously minimizing fan energy (which scales with the cube of speed per the affinity laws — see `psychrometrics.md`) while avoiding starving any zone ([KU Leuven, *Energy & Buildings 174, 2018*](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/513659)).
- The duct static pressure sensor location matters: placing it too far downstream/near the fan (rather than at a hydraulically representative remote point) forces a higher setpoint than necessary and wastes fan energy — directly analogous to the DP sensor placement guidance for chilled-water plants.

**T&R parameter starting points** (tune to the specific system — these are typical starting increments, not universal constants): trim-up increment small (e.g., 0.02–0.05 in. wg per cycle) so the setpoint doesn't overshoot on a single request; trim-down decrement larger (e.g., 0.05–0.1 in. wg per cycle when zero requests persist for several minutes) so the system aggressively seeks the floor when it can. Always bound with a hard minimum and maximum SP setpoint.

## VAV Terminal Boxes

- **Pressure-independent control**: Modern VAV boxes use an airflow sensor (typically a flow cross or similar averaging pickup) in a cascade arrangement — an outer loop (zone temperature) sets an airflow setpoint (cfm), and an inner loop (airflow) modulates the damper to hold that cfm setpoint regardless of upstream duct static pressure fluctuations. This decouples zone comfort control from duct pressure variation across the system, unlike older pressure-dependent (damper-position-only) boxes.
- **Minimum airflow setpoints** are governed by the greater of (a) code-required ventilation (ASHRAE 62.1 people/area components), (b) a heating-mode minimum sufficient to deliver reheat capacity without excessive discharge temperature, and (c) an "occupied standby" or unoccupied minimum during setback. Reheat coils are typically sequenced to modulate only once the box is at its heating minimum airflow.

## Dedicated Outdoor Air Systems (DOAS)

DOAS units decouple ventilation air conditioning from space sensible cooling/heating, delivering pre-conditioned outdoor air directly to spaces or to local terminal units, while a separate system (e.g., fan coils, VRF, radiant) handles the bulk of the sensible load ([Trane, *DOAS Configurations and Control Strategies*](https://www.trane.com/content/dam/Trane/Commercial/global/learning-center/ashrae-articles/DOAS%20Configurations%20and%20Control%20Strategies.pdf)).

- **"Cold, not neutral" delivery**: Many DOAS designs dehumidify OA to a dew point below the space setpoint, then deliver it *cold* (rather than reheating to neutral), using the DOAS's inherent dehumidification capacity to help offset the space's latent load — this simplifies local unit control because the local unit only manages sensible cooling/heating, not moisture removal ([Trane, DOAS Configurations](https://www.trane.com/content/dam/Trane/Commercial/global/learning-center/ashrae-articles/DOAS%20Configurations%20and%20Control%20Strategies.pdf)).
- **Constant dew-point control**: A common DOAS control approach targets a constant leaving dew point (not exceeding a setpoint) and a constant (often neutral or slightly-cool) dry-bulb, allowing the DOAS to run independently of zone-by-zone load variation ([Trane, DOAS Configurations](https://www.trane.com/content/dam/Trane/Commercial/global/learning-center/ashrae-articles/DOAS%20Configurations%20and%20Control%20Strategies.pdf)).

**Sequence note**: because the DOAS is deliberately supplying "cold" air rather than neutral air, make sure the local terminal unit (FCU/VRF) sequence expects a cold ventilation contribution in its sensible cooling calc — otherwise the space can overcool or the local unit's cooling stage can be miscalibrated against actual total load.

## Energy Recovery (Wheels, Plate Heat Exchangers)

- **Enthalpy (total energy recovery) wheels** transfer both sensible heat and moisture between exhaust and outdoor air streams, recovering 60–80% of the heating/cooling energy that would otherwise be lost to exhaust — a major DOAS and campus ventilation-load reduction strategy ([ingener.by, *Educational Facilities HVAC Systems*](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/educational-facilities/)).
- **Cross-contamination risk**: Rotary wheels physically rotate through both airstreams, creating a small but real potential for cross-contamination/carryover between exhaust and supply air — a significant concern in labs, healthcare, and any application where exhaust air quality is a hazard concern; purge sections and appropriate seal design mitigate but don't eliminate this risk ([ECE UK, *Thermal Wheels in Healthcare*](https://www.eceuk.com/knowledge-hub/blog/should-thermal-wheels-be-used-in-healthcare/)).
- **Plate (fixed-plate) heat exchangers** provide sensible-only (or, with specialized membranes, some latent) energy transfer with **no moving parts and no direct airstream mixing**, making them the preferred energy-recovery technology for laboratory exhaust and other applications where cross-contamination must be strictly avoided.

**Selection rule of thumb**: default to enthalpy wheels for general ventilation air (offices, classrooms, dorms) where recovering latent load matters and cross-contamination risk is low; default to fixed-plate exchangers for lab exhaust or any application where the exhaust airstream could carry hazardous contaminants.

## Fan Coil Units (FCUs)

FCUs — typically 2-pipe or 4-pipe hydronic units serving individual zones (common in dorms, hotels, and older campus buildings) — provide localized sensible (and sometimes latent) conditioning from a central chilled/hot water loop, with local thermostatic or DDC control of the water valve and fan speed. In higher-ed retrofit contexts, FCUs are frequently retained for zone-level control while ventilation air is supplied by a separate DOAS, decoupling the ventilation and comfort-conditioning functions ([Consulting-Specifying Engineer, *HVAC design considerations for unique university spaces*](https://www.csemag.com/hvac-design-considerations-and-solutions-for-unique-university-spaces/)).

## Lab Exhaust / Fume Hood Controls and Pressurization

Laboratory buildings on higher-ed science campuses require some of the most sophisticated airside controls in commercial building automation, governed by ASHRAE 110 (hood performance testing) and ANSI/ASSP Z9.5 (laboratory ventilation system design). Key controls concepts:

- **Face velocity control**: VAV fume hoods maintain a target face velocity (commonly 80–120 fpm per Z9.5 guidance, with 100 fpm most frequently specified) by modulating an exhaust valve/damper as the operator raises or lowers the sash, using a sash-position sensor or direct face-velocity feedback ([fumehoods.info, *ANSI/AIHA Z9.5*](https://www.fumehoods.info/blog/ansiaiha-z95-understanding-the-laboratory-ventilation-standard-mmdwwmk8); [TSI, *Quick Guide to Z9.5-2022*](https://tsi.com/life-sciences/learn/quick-guide-to-ansi-assp-z9-5-2022-laboratory-ventilation)).
- **Minimum exhaust volume**: Even with the sash closed, VAV hoods must maintain a minimum flow (Z9.5 recommends not less than 25 cfm/ft² of hood work surface, or ~1 L/s/m²) to keep contaminants diluted and away from re-entry — Z9.5-2022 replaced the older single air-change-rate rule with a recommended (non-binding) ACH range of 150–375, requiring the design team's own hazard assessment ([DCHAS-L Archive](https://www.ilpi.com/dchas/2013/20130211e.html); [NIH ORF, *Manifold Exhaust Requirements & Z9.5-2022*](https://orf.od.nih.gov/TechnicalResources/Documents/News%20to%20Use%20PDF%20Files/2022%20NTU/Manifold%20Exhaust%20Requirements%20and%20ANSI%20ASSP%20Z9.5-2022%20-%20November%202022%20News%20to%20Use_508.pdf)).
- **Room-level minimum ACH**: Z9.5-2022 introduced a numerical general lab ventilation rate of **4–10 ACH**, replacing the previous "per owner specification" placeholder ([TSI, *Quick Guide to Z9.5-2022*](https://tsi.com/life-sciences/learn/quick-guide-to-ansi-assp-z9-5-2022-laboratory-ventilation)).
- **Room/building pressurization**: Labs are maintained at negative pressure relative to corridors/adjacent non-lab spaces via a supply/exhaust airflow offset (supply cfm set some fixed or tracked amount below total exhaust cfm), preventing contaminant migration out of the lab. Building-level pressurization is cascaded: labs negative to corridors, corridors typically neutral-to-slightly-positive relative to outdoors. Pressure-independent airflow control (flow tracking, not static-pressure/position-only control) is standard for both supply and general exhaust valves so that room offset is maintained even as individual fume hoods modulate ([NIH ORF, *Manifold Exhaust Requirements*](https://orf.od.nih.gov/TechnicalResources/Documents/News%20to%20Use%20PDF%20Files/2022%20NTU/Manifold%20Exhaust%20Requirements%20and%20ANSI%20ASSP%20Z9.5-2022%20-%20November%202022%20News%20to%20Use_508.pdf)).
- **Manifolded exhaust**: Multiple hoods/general exhaust points can share a common exhaust riser/fan system provided each branch has its own pressure-independent flow-regulating device and all combined airstreams are chemically compatible (below LEL, non-reactive) — common in multi-story lab buildings to reduce rooftop fan count, with future-capacity allowance built into risers ([NIH ORF](https://orf.od.nih.gov/TechnicalResources/Documents/News%20to%20Use%20PDF%20Files/2022%20NTU/Manifold%20Exhaust%20Requirements%20and%20ANSI%20ASSP%20Z9.5-2022%20-%20November%202022%20News%20to%20Use_508.pdf)).
- **Fans run continuously**: Lab exhaust fans are required to run continuously, independent of any local switch or time clock at the hood, and hoods must not have a local on/off switch — life-safety-driven design and control practice ([Stanford EH&S, *Fume Hood Construction, Installation & Performance*](https://ehs.stanford.edu/manual/laboratory-standard-design-guidelines/fume-hood-construction-installation-performance)).
- **Sash management / VAV energy savings**: Because exhaust volume tracks sash opening, VAV fume hood controls (paired with occupancy-based sash-closure reminders/interlocks) are a major energy-reduction lever in lab buildings, given how energy-intensive 100%-outside-air, once-through lab ventilation is.

### Lab Ventilation Standards Detail (ASHRAE 110 / ANSI Z9.5)

- **ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 110-2016 (R2025)** — *Methods of Testing Performance of Laboratory Fume Hoods* — is the American National Standard specifying quantitative and qualitative test methods for fume hood containment ([The ANSI Blog, *ANSI/ASHRAE 110*](https://blog.ansi.org/ansi/ansi-ashrae-110-testing-laboratory-fume-hoods/)). It defines three test procedures ([GZ Lab Furniture, *ASHRAE 110 Fume Hood Performance Test Standard Explained*](https://www.gzlabfurniture.com/ashrae-110-fume-hood-performacne-test-standard/)):
  1. **Face velocity measurement** — grid-based anemometer readings across the hood opening, at 25%/50%/full sash for VAV hoods.
  2. **Face velocity/airflow visualization** — smoke or similar visualization of containment/turbulence.
  3. **Tracer gas containment test** — SF₆ gas released inside the hood at 4.0 L/min, with a breathing-zone probe at a mannequin measuring leakage (commonly reported as an "AM" — as-manufactured — rating, with 4.0 AM 0.05 or better cited as achievable for well-designed hoods).
  OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1450 and ANSI/AIHA Z9.5 both reference ASHRAE 110 as the benchmark validation method for fume hood performance ([GZ Lab Furniture](https://www.gzlabfurniture.com/ashrae-110-fume-hood-performacne-test-standard/); [ANSI Blog, *Laboratory Ventilation Package*](https://blog.ansi.org/ansi/lab-ventilation-ansi-aiha-asse-z9-5-ashrae-110/)).
- **ANSI/ASSP Z9.5-2022** (formerly ANSI/AIHA Z9.5) — *Laboratory Ventilation* — is the comprehensive design/operations standard (150+ pages, 10 sections, 450+ requirements/good practices) covering the Laboratory Ventilation Management Plan (LVMP), fume hoods and other containment devices (gloveboxes, ductless hoods, perchloric-acid hoods), system design, commissioning and routine performance testing, work practices, preventive maintenance, and air cleaning ([AIHA/Synergist, *The New Laboratory Ventilation Standard*](https://publications.aiha.org/20220607-lab-ventilation-standard)). Key numerical provisions relevant to controls engineers:
  - Face velocity: 80–120 fpm typical range (100 fpm most commonly targeted), with ±20 fpm allowed deviation across the face ([fumehoods.info](https://www.fumehoods.info/blog/ansiaiha-z95-understanding-the-laboratory-ventilation-standard-mmdwwmk8)).
  - Minimum VAV hood exhaust when sash closed: not less than ~25 cfm/ft² of hood work surface (1 L/s/m²) — a **minimum flow floor**, distinct from face-velocity control, that must be programmed into VAV hood logic.
  - General lab room ventilation: **4–10 ACH** occupied minimum (2022 revision added this numeric value, replacing the earlier vague "per owner spec"); non-binding overall interior ACH guidance range of 150–375 depending on hazard assessment.
  - Makeup air = 100% of exhaust volume, with the lab held at slight negative pressure relative to corridors/adjacent spaces.
  - Exhaust discharge above roof level to prevent re-entrainment.
  - Flow/face-velocity alarms required on all hoods; annual certification and testing required.
  - Manifolding of fume-hood exhaust into shared risers/fans is permitted if each branch has pressure-independent flow control and all combined airstreams are chemically compatible (below LEL, non-reactive) ([NIH ORF, *Manifold Exhaust Requirements and Z9.5-2022*](https://orf.od.nih.gov/TechnicalResources/Documents/News%20to%20Use%20PDF%20Files/2022%20NTU/Manifold%20Exhaust%20Requirements%20and%20ANSI%20ASSP%20Z9.5-2022%20-%20November%202022%20News%20to%20Use_508.pdf)).

These two standards, paired as the "ANSI/AIHA/ASSE Z9.5 and ANSI/ASHRAE 110 Laboratory Ventilation Package," are the primary code-adjacent references governing controls design, sequencing, commissioning, and ongoing performance verification (including BAS-reported data) for any higher-ed science, engineering, or medical-research building's exhaust and pressurization systems ([The ANSI Blog, *Laboratory Ventilation Package*](https://blog.ansi.org/ansi/lab-ventilation-ansi-aiha-asse-z9-5-ashrae-110/)).

## Dorm, Classroom, and Auditorium Load Characteristics

| Space type | People OA (cfm/person) | Area OA (cfm/ft²) | Typical SHR | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Classrooms (age 9+) | 10 | 0.12 | ~0.8–0.85 | Combined default ~13–14 cfm/person at typical density ([ingener.by](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/educational-facilities/)) |
| Libraries | 5 | 0.12 | Higher latent sensitivity | Requires tight humidity control for materials preservation (see below) |
| Laboratories | 10 | 0.18 | Governed by exhaust, not comfort load | Ventilation set by safety code, not thermal comfort |
| Gymnasiums | 20 | 0.06 | Low SHR at high occupancy | Large, intermittent occupancy swings |
| Auditoriums / high-density assembly | — | — | ~0.45–0.65 | Extreme load diversity; peak design loads occur <5% of operating hours; often good candidates for displacement ventilation ([ingener.by, *High-Density Occupancy HVAC*](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/high-occupancy-density-hvac/); [Consulting-Specifying Engineer](https://www.csemag.com/hvac-design-considerations-and-solutions-for-unique-university-spaces/)) |
| Dormitories | Residential-type diversity | — | Typically FCU or 4-pipe fan coil / VRF served | Non-coincident scheduling — occupant presence and internal loads vary sharply by time of day and academic calendar |

**Displacement ventilation** is frequently recommended for tall, high-volume spaces like auditoriums and libraries: low-velocity, near-floor-level, moderate-temperature supply air conditions only the occupied zone (typically the lowest ~6 ft), letting stratified air above rise in temperature — reducing total cooling energy and improving contaminant control relative to fully-mixed systems ([Consulting-Specifying Engineer, *HVAC design considerations and solutions for unique university spaces*](https://www.csemag.com/hvac-design-considerations-and-solutions-for-unique-university-spaces/)).

**Demand-controlled ventilation (DCV)** using CO₂ sensors is standard practice for classrooms and auditoriums with highly variable occupancy, reducing outdoor-air volumes 30–50% relative to constant-volume ventilation while maintaining code-required IAQ, with CO₂ setpoints typically in the 1,000–1,100 ppm range ([ingener.by, *High-Density Occupancy HVAC*](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/high-occupancy-density-hvac/)).

## Humidity Control in Libraries/Archives

Special-collection libraries, archives, and museum-adjacent campus spaces require tight, stable humidity control distinct from ordinary comfort conditioning:

- **Typical target bands**: 40–55% RH and 68–72°F for mixed collections; the Smithsonian recommends a broader 30–50% RH, 35–65°F band specifically for paper archives ([Climico HVAC, *Museum & Archive HVAC*](https://climicohvac.com/blog/museum-archives-hvac-requirements/)).
- **Rate-of-change limits, not just absolute setpoints, are the binding design constraint** — many guidance documents (e.g., the Getty Conservation Institute's "±5/±5" standard) emphasize stability over an exact number: daily swings should stay within ±4°F and ±5% RH, and rate-of-change should not exceed roughly 5% RH per month for the tightest archival applications ([Climico HVAC](https://climicohvac.com/blog/museum-archives-hvac-requirements/); [ingener.by, *HVAC Precision Control for Museum Collections*](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/museums-galleries-archives-libraries/temperature-humidity-control-preservation/)).
- **Design/controls practices**: dedicated air-handling units per micro-climate zone (separating display/storage areas from public and office spaces); low-velocity laminar supply (roughly 200–300 fpm) to avoid localized RH gradients; return-air sensing with an accounted mixing-time delay; redundant, high-accuracy (±1% RH) capacitive sensors with NIST-traceable calibration; gradual unoccupied setback limited to about 2°F/hour and 2%RH/hour rather than aggressive night setback; and 24/7 operation with N+1 redundancy for the most sensitive archival vaults ([ingener.by, *HVAC Precision Control for Museum Collections*](https://ingener.by/specialty-applications-testing/specialty-hvac-applications/museums-galleries-archives-libraries/temperature-humidity-control-preservation/)).
- **Energy recovery + Guideline 36-style reset**: energy-recovery wheels can capture up to ~70% of exhaust moisture to precondition incoming air, and ASHRAE Guideline 36-style reset sequences help avoid the AHU working harder than needed while maintaining tight RH tolerance ([Climico HVAC](https://climicohvac.com/blog/museum-archives-hvac-requirements/)).

## Higher-Ed Campus Operations Context

- **Submetering**: Campus-wide BAS/energy-management programs typically layer building- or department-level submeters atop the campus utility meter, enabling targeted retro-commissioning, demand-response participation, and utility bill allocation across departments; metering granularity should be chosen to match the institution's reduction goals, budget, and data-management capacity rather than a one-size-fits-all approach ([Southface Institute, *Measuring Campus Performance — Submetering Bulletin*](http://www.southface.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/Submetering-bulletin.pdf)).
- **Scheduling and diversity**: Because academic buildings (classrooms, labs, dorms, auditoriums) have sharply different and non-coincident occupancy patterns across the day and academic calendar, campus-wide BAS scheduling exploits **load diversity** — the fact that peak demand for the whole campus is meaningfully lower than the sum of individual building peaks — to size central plants smaller than the sum of building peaks would otherwise require, and to shift TES charging/discharging windows around the aggregate campus load curve rather than any single building's schedule ([University of Illinois, *Central Chilled Water System TES*](https://docs.fs.illinois.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/Thermal_Energy_Storage.pdf)). Optimal-start/stop algorithms, informed by building thermal mass and weather, further reduce unnecessary early-morning plant ramp-up across a diverse building portfolio.
- **Unoccupied-hours focus**: Because campus buildings often accumulate thousands of unoccupied hours per year (some references cite 6,000+ hours annually for certain academic buildings), the most effective energy strategies concentrate setback, ventilation reduction, and scheduling optimization on unoccupied periods rather than compromising occupied-hour ventilation rates ([ASHRAE, *Design Guidance for Education Facilities v2.0*](https://www.ashrae.org/file%20library/technical%20resources/free%20resources/design-guidance-for-education-facilities_version-2.0.pdf)).

## Common Mistakes (Airside)

- Using OAT-only SAT reset when zone/coil valve-demand-based T&R is available — OAT-only is simpler but leaves savings on the table and can overshoot on shoulder-season days when internal load and OAT disagree.
- Placing the duct static pressure sensor near the AHU discharge instead of a hydraulically representative remote point — silently inflates the SP floor and wastes fan energy at the cube-law rate.
- Specifying an enthalpy wheel for lab exhaust energy recovery without checking cross-contamination risk — use a fixed-plate exchanger where exhaust air quality is a hazard concern.
- Treating fume hood face-velocity control as the only exhaust control needed — forgetting the Z9.5 minimum-flow floor (≥25 cfm/ft²) at closed sash leaves the hood non-compliant even though face velocity reads fine at open sash.
- Applying a single generic humidity deadband to a library/archive space — rate-of-change limits (±5/±5 or tighter) are the actual binding constraint, not just the absolute RH band.
