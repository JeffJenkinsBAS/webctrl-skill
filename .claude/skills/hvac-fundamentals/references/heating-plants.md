# Heating Plants Reference

Deep reference for boiler efficiency curves, hot water reset, campus steam, and heat-recovery/district-energy systems. Read alongside the Reset Strategy Guidance table in SKILL.md.

## Condensing vs. Non-Condensing Boilers

- **Non-condensing boilers**: Roughly 81–88% efficient; combustion flue gas is vented above its dew point (~130°F for natural gas, ~122°F for propane) to avoid corrosive condensate forming inside a heat exchanger not designed to handle it. Return water temperature must be kept **above ~130–140°F** to protect the boiler from internal condensation and corrosion ([JMP Co, *Boiler Installation Pitfalls: Never Condense a Non-Condensing Boiler*](https://jmpcoblog.com/hvac-blog/boiler-installation-pitfalls-part-1-never-condense-a-non-condensing-boiler); [Maddock Industries](https://maddockindustries.com/hydronics-blog/condensing-vs-non-condensing-boilers-the-case-for-hybrid-systems/)).
- **Condensing boilers**: Use corrosion-resistant heat exchangers (typically stainless steel or aluminum alloys) specifically designed to allow flue gas condensation, recovering the latent heat of combustion moisture (roughly 10% of total heat) that non-condensing units simply vent to atmosphere. Efficiency reaches 92–99%, **but only when return water temperature is kept below ~130°F** — the condensing "TE" (thermal efficiency) curve drops sharply as return water temperature rises above the flue-gas dew point ([ENERGY STAR, *Commercial Boiler Design Guide*](https://www.energystar.gov/sites/default/files/asset/document/ENERGY%20STAR%20Commercial%20Boiler%20Design%20Guide.pdf)).
- **Controls implication**: A very common commissioning failure is installing a condensing boiler but operating it at legacy non-condensing hot-water reset curves/return temperatures (e.g., 180°F supply / 160°F return) — one industry estimate cited is that ~90% of commercial condensing boilers never actually operate in their condensing range because of this ([ENERGY STAR](https://www.energystar.gov/sites/default/files/asset/document/ENERGY%20STAR%20Commercial%20Boiler%20Design%20Guide.pdf)). Controls engineers must ensure the outdoor-air reset curve and terminal-unit ΔT design genuinely drive return water below ~130°F for a meaningful fraction of the heating season.
- **Turndown**: ENERGY STAR-certified condensing boilers require a minimum 5:1 turndown ratio; modern high-efficiency modulating condensing units achieve 3:1 to 25:1, in 1% firing-rate increments, enabling load-matching rather than on/off cycling ([squarespace hosted PDF, *Condensing & Non-Condensing Boilers/Water Heaters*](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/556c6ecae4b099a4afb2aa99/556c8b72e4b0c6e620556497/556c8b72e4b0c6e6205565ce/1420731698047/Condensing_vs_NonCondensing.pdf)).
- **Hybrid boiler plants**: Combine condensing and non-condensing (or high-mass/low-mass) boilers so the condensing unit(s) lead for most of the season while the non-condensing unit handles the coldest days when supply temperature must run high; sequencing logic typically switches based on return water temperature crossing ~130–135°F ([Maddock Industries](https://maddockindustries.com/hydronics-blog/condensing-vs-non-condensing-boilers-the-case-for-hybrid-systems/)).

**Quick diagnostic**: pull the boiler return water temperature trend across a full heating season. If it rarely or never dips below ~130°F, the reset curve is wrong (or terminal ΔT is too narrow) and the plant is paying non-condensing energy costs for condensing-boiler hardware.

## Hot Water Reset

Outdoor-air (OA) reset of hot-water supply temperature (HWST) is the primary strategy for maximizing condensing-boiler efficiency and minimizing distribution losses: as OA temperature rises, HWST setpoint is lowered along a reset schedule (heating curve), typically bounded by a maximum design HWST at design OA temperature and a minimum HWST at some warmer OA cutoff. Every 1°F of hot water reset (or thermostat setback) is commonly cited as yielding roughly proportional heating-energy savings, and colder-climate systems see the largest gains ([HeatLink, *Heating Curves and Reset Ratios*](https://www.heatlink.com/sites/default/files/TechNote/TN14-Heating-Curves-and-Reset-Ratios-2005-10-06.pdf); [DOE Building America, *Measure Guideline: Condensing Boilers*](https://www1.eere.energy.gov/buildings/publications/pdfs/building_america/measure_guide_condensing_boilers.pdf)). Increasing distribution ΔT (e.g., from a legacy 20°F design to a wider spread) reduces required flow and pumping energy per unit of heat delivered — the water-side analog of low-ΔT/high-ΔT air-side design tradeoffs ([ENERGY STAR](https://www.energystar.gov/sites/default/files/asset/document/ENERGY%20STAR%20Commercial%20Boiler%20Design%20Guide.pdf)).

**Reset curve sequence checklist:**
1. Set design HWST at design (coldest) OA temperature (commonly 180°F, but verify against the condensing boiler's condensing threshold — lower design temps let the boiler condense more often).
2. Set minimum HWST at a warm-OA cutoff (e.g., 100–120°F at 60°F+ OA), floor-limited by terminal-unit capacity at minimum flow.
3. Interpolate linearly (or per a documented curve) between those two points based on measured OA temperature.
4. Cross-check against a trim-and-respond overlay if available — if terminal valves aren't near-100% open, HWST can often be reset even lower than the fixed OA curve alone would suggest, same principle as CHWST reset.
5. Confirm the resulting return-water temperature trend actually clears the condensing threshold across the season (see diagnostic above).

## Steam Systems on Campuses

Many older university campuses retain central steam plants feeding a distribution loop to dozens of buildings. A representative configuration (Morgan State University case study): a central heating plant with multiple firetube steam boilers, a deaerator (DA) with surge tank, feedwater/condensate transfer pumps, blowdown separator/heat exchanger, and centralized controls/metering; steam is distributed at roughly 100 psi to building services, where a **pressure-reducing valve (PRV) station** drops it to low-pressure steam for building heat exchangers that produce hot water for terminal heating ([Steam Management Inc., *Morgan State University Case Study*](https://www.steammgt.com/msu-casestudy)). Controls-relevant issues on campus steam systems include steam trap failure/leakage (a major source of energy loss and a frequent retro-commissioning target), condensate return integrity, and insulation losses on distribution piping — all commonly instrumented and trended via campus BAS/metering programs.

**BAS monitoring points worth trending on campus steam**: PRV station inlet/outlet pressure (confirms PRV isn't stuck open or failing), steam trap discharge temperature (failed-open traps run continuously hot; failed-closed traps back up condensate), condensate return flow/temperature versus expected supply steam flow (a large mismatch flags line leaks or unreturned condensate), and building heat exchanger approach temperature (widening approach over time flags fouling).

## Heat Pumps, Heat Recovery Chillers, and District Energy Loops

- **Heat recovery chillers** reject condenser heat to a *second* condenser bundle feeding a heating hot-water loop (instead of, or in addition to, a cooling tower), effectively functioning as a large water-to-water heat pump that simultaneously produces chilled water and useful heat. This is one of the most impactful decarbonization/efficiency retrofits available on campuses with simultaneous heating and cooling demand (common in labs and data-center-adjacent buildings) ([Site.Google/LBL, *Green Clean & Mean — Waste Heat Recovery*](https://sites.google.com/a/lbl.gov/green-clean-mean/key-strategies/waste-heat-recovery)).
- **Lab exhaust heat recovery + heat-recovery-chiller integration**: A documented university project added an energy recovery loop on laboratory exhaust and reconfigured chillers into heat-recovery mode, pulling heat from the campus chilled-water loop to heat dehumidification coils — cutting steam use 25% and reducing chilled-water consumption by over 67,000 ton-hours annually ([District Energy Association, *Energy Savings Realized Upon Completion of CST Project*](https://www.districtenergy.org/blogs/district-energy/2024/03/26/energy-savings-realized-upon-completion-of-cst-pro)).
- **Low-temperature hot-water (LTHW) district loops**: Designing the campus heating-loop temperature low enough (e.g., 120–140°F) allows heat-recovery chillers to feed the loop directly without an intermediate heat-pump boost stage, substantially improving capital and operating economics of waste-heat capture ([LBL, *Waste Heat Recovery*](https://sites.google.com/a/lbl.gov/green-clean-mean/key-strategies/waste-heat-recovery)).
- **Campus-scale electrification examples**: Stanford's district energy system uses heat-recovery chillers, hot-water generators, and storage tanks to handle demand spikes while reducing water and steam use ([Caccia Plumbing, *How Stanford's District Energy System Changes Building Plumbing*](https://cacciaplumbing.com/blog/how-stanfords-district-energy-system-works/); [Stanford, *deChalendar & Benson, HICCS 2019*](https://web.stanford.edu/~glynn/papers/2019/deChalendarGBenson2019.pdf)). Western University's district energy retrofit added heat recovery from its steam plant into the chilled-water network (repurposed as a campus-wide "energy loop"), plus new electric boilers producing ~40% of campus steam and cutting emissions 20–30% ([Western University Sustainability](https://sustainability.uwo.ca/Campus/energy_carbon/district_energy_system.html)).

**Sequence-writing implication for heat recovery chillers**: because the unit is simultaneously satisfying a cooling load and a heating load, the control priority logic must decide which load "leads" (usually cooling load leads on the evaporator side, and the heat-recovery condenser modulates/dumps excess heat to a standard tower when the heating loop can't absorb everything the compressor produces) — treat it as two coupled loops, not one.

## Common Mistakes (Heating Plants)

- Leaving a condensing boiler on a non-condensing-era reset curve (180°F supply/160°F return) — verify actual condensing operation via return-temperature trend, not nameplate efficiency.
- Assuming steam trap health from age alone — trend discharge temperature/condensate flow; failed traps are a leading unmetered energy loss on campus steam systems.
- Designing LTHW heat-recovery integration without checking whether the existing distribution loop temperature is low enough for the heat-recovery chiller to feed it directly — an intermediate boost stage adds cost and complexity that a lower design loop temperature could avoid.
